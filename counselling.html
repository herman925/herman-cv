The Origin

From Struggle to Support

My journey into counseling began in Year 7 as an international school student. Like many teenagers, I faced significant mental health challenges. It was a turbulent time where I felt lost and misunderstood.

I found my lifeline in Drama, Music, and Peer Counseling. These weren't just activities; they were safe harbours. I met mentors who offered unconditional support, but I also experienced betrayal and criticism from others who dismissed me as "incompetent."

That duality—being both held and hurt—stayed with me. It followed me through university where I studied Psychology, then into my postgraduate training in Guidance and Counselling. Each step was an attempt to understand what I had lived through, and to become someone who could offer what I once desperately needed.

"That pain became my purpose. The experience of being both supported and judged shaped my worldview. It fueled a deep, burning desire to understand people—to be the empathetic adult I needed when I was younger. Years later, when I walked into ISS to work with refugees, I finally understood why."

The Crucible

International Social Services

My time at ISS (Hong Kong Branch) was a defining chapter. I worked directly with non-refoulement claimants (refugees), witnessing raw human suffering—fear, danger, and the struggle for survival without a safety net.

This experience was a masterclass in cultural humility. I learned to navigate diverse values on family, politics, and the future, understanding how trauma manifests differently across cultures. It deepened my capacity to sit with pain and offer stability in the midst of chaos.

But I also faced a harsh reality: paperwork was drowning us. Administrative burdens consumed hours that should have been spent with clients. I made a choice—to learn technology not for its own sake, but to reclaim the space and energy my clients deserved. Every system I built, every hour automated, was an hour returned to human connection.
